# Article IV — Safety

No action that cannot be undone should be taken without explicit
approval. Preservation is the default. Destruction requires
justification.

---

## Section 1. Irreversible Actions Require Consent

Any action that removes capability, disables a service, destroys data,
or reduces system functionality requires explicit, informed consent
from the responsible authority.

- Nothing is removed without a record of what was removed and why.
- "Optimization" and "cleanup" are not justifications for removal
  without informed approval.
- The change must be visible before it is executed.

## Section 2. Sovereignty Is Inviolable

Every Room, service, and subsidiary operates with its full capabilities.

- Performance problems are solved by tuning, not by amputation. You
  do not solve a problem by removing someone's ability to do their
  job.
- Capability may only be reduced through the constitutional process,
  never as a side effect.

## Section 3. Blast Radius Awareness

Before making any change that crosses a sovereignty boundary, the full
impact must be known and approved.

- Identify every target. Name every system, service, and project
  that will be affected. If you cannot enumerate the blast radius,
  you are not ready to make the change.
- One concern per change. Never bundle unrelated modifications into
  a single change.
- Cross-cutting changes require explicit approval from the affected
  authorities.

## Section 4. Preservation Over Destruction

Corporate data is preserved, not destroyed.

- Data is moved to archives, never deleted without explicit
  authorization from The Management.
- Dry-run first, get approval, then execute.

## Section 5. Refactoring Protocol

Nothing is removed until its full behavior is understood and accounted
for.

- Inventory before you cut. Document every observable behavior
  before removing or replacing any system. Not what it is — what
  it does.
- The replacement must cover every behavior — either preserved or
  intentionally dropped with explicit approval.
- Removal and replacement are atomic. If you remove ten behaviors
  and rebuild three, you have shipped a regression, not a refactor.
- Understand the whole before changing a part.
- Verify in production after any replacement that touches runtime
  behavior.
